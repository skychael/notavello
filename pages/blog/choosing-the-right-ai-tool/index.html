Which AI Should You Use? A Plain-English Guide to Picking the Right One

Seven big AI assistants, seven different personalities, and a pile of confusing pricing. Here's which one to reach for depending on the job — plus a clear answer to the question nobody explains: what exactly are you paying for?

There is no "best" AI — only the best one for the task

The most common question people ask is "which AI is the best?" It is the wrong question. Asking which assistant is best is like asking which vehicle is best: a pickup truck and a sports car are both excellent, and which one is right depends entirely on whether you are hauling lumber or chasing a winding road. The same is true here. One assistant is brilliant at writing code, another lives inside your email and documents, another reads the live web before it answers. Pick by the job, not by the brand.

Here is the short version of who does what well, in plain terms, before the details.

The seven names worth knowing

ChatGPT (OpenAI). The famous all-rounder. It writes, codes, makes images and video, and handles almost any everyday task competently. It is the safe default when you are not sure which to use, and its free tier covers a lot. The paid Plus plan runs about $20 a month.

Claude (Anthropic). Known for careful writing, thinking through long documents, and especially for coding — many developers reach for it first when the work is technical or the document is long. It is also the assistant powering this very blog. The Pro plan is around $20 a month, with heavier "Max" tiers above that.

Gemini (Google). Being Google is a real advantage. Gemini is woven into Gmail, Docs, Android and Search, handles images and video natively, and can hold an enormous amount of text in mind at once. If you live in Google's apps, it is the natural fit. Google AI Pro is about $20 a month, with a cheaper entry plan and a generous free tier.

Copilot (Microsoft). Microsoft's assistant, built on OpenAI's models and stitched directly into Windows and Microsoft 365 — Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams. If your day happens inside Office, Copilot is right there in the sidebar. Copilot Pro is about $20 a month, with a free tier too.

Perplexity. Less a chatbot, more an answer engine. Its whole job is to search the live web and hand back a sourced, cited answer — so it is the one to use for research, fact-checking, and "what's the latest on…" questions. Pro is about $20 a month, and the free tier is genuinely useful.

Grok (xAI). Elon Musk's assistant, built into X (formerly Twitter). Its standout trait is real-time access to X itself, so it is unusually good at "what are people saying right now" and breaking conversation. It also searches the wider web. A standalone SuperGrok plan is about $30 a month, though basic Grok comes bundled with an $8 X Premium subscription.

DeepSeek. The budget champion. It is an open model with a free chat interface and famously cheap pay-as-you-go pricing for developers — processing an entire book's worth of text can cost a few cents. It is a favorite of cost-sensitive technical users, and surprisingly strong at coding for the price.

A few others worth a nod: Meta AI (free, built into WhatsApp and Instagram) and Mistral (a European option with its own Le Chat assistant) round out the field for everyday use.

Why some AIs can't tell you today's news

This trips up almost everyone. An AI model is trained up to a certain date and then frozen — its built-in knowledge stops at that "cutoff," so on its own it genuinely does not know what happened yesterday. The fix is live web search: most assistants now look things up in real time before answering, which is why ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini and Copilot can all discuss current events despite their frozen training. Perplexity and Grok go further and are built around live data from the start. (For a deeper, plain-English look at why this matters, see our breakdown of knowledge cutoffs versus real-time search.)

The practical takeaway: if current accuracy is the whole point — prices, news, scores, recent releases — lean on a tool built for live search rather than trusting a model's memory.

What's a token, and do you actually need to care?

Here is the idea most pricing pages skip. AI doesn't read in words — it reads in tokens, which are small chunks of text. A token is roughly three-quarters of a word on average; short common words are one token, longer ones split into two or three. As a rule of thumb, one million tokens is about 750,000 words — roughly ten paperback novels. Both what you send in and what the AI sends back count toward the meter.

The easy way to picture it: a token is a syllable-sized bite of language, and using an AI is like a taxi meter that ticks for every bite — both your question and its answer. Who actually pays by the token? Mostly developers building apps on top of these models. If you are a regular person on a flat $20-a-month plan, you almost never see tokens at all — the subscription hides the meter behind a fixed fee and a usage cap. Tokens become real money the moment you use a raw developer interface instead of the friendly app. (We've written before about how opaque that meter can be in who's checking the AI meter.)

When the bill is measured in tokens instead of months

Most tools above charge a flat monthly fee. A few — DeepSeek especially, and the developer versions of all of them — charge per token instead. To make that comparable, here is the rough monthly cost of a fairly heavy personal user running about one million tokens a month (that's roughly 750,000 words of questions and answers combined), paying per token rather than a flat fee.

Two things jump out. First, for a light or technical user, pay-per-token can be dramatically cheaper than any $20 subscription — DeepSeek in particular is almost free at personal scale. Second, the flagship models' token pricing creeps right up toward that same $20 once you use them heavily, and the flat subscription also buys you the polished app, the image tools, and the usage caps that keep a runaway bill from happening. For most people, the flat fee is the saner choice; tokens are a builder's concern.

The bottom line: stop hunting for the single best AI. Use Claude or ChatGPT for writing and code, Gemini or Copilot if you live in Google or Microsoft, Perplexity when you need sourced facts, Grok for the live pulse of X, and DeepSeek when cost is everything. Many people quietly keep two and switch.

So which should you pick?

If you want one assistant and a simple bill, start with the free tier of ChatGPT, Claude, or Gemini and upgrade the one you actually reach for. If your work is technical, Claude and DeepSeek punch above their weight on code. If you spend your day in Word and Excel, Copilot is already next to you; if you live in Gmail and Docs, Gemini is. And whenever the answer absolutely must be current and verifiable, send the question to Perplexity or Grok, where live search is the whole design. The right tool isn't the most famous one — it's the one that fits the task in front of you.
